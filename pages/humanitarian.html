The Field Before the Academy

Where the Law Meets the Ground

Before Dr. Nyawo became a published scholar, he spent more than a decade in the field — in Angola, Northern Uganda, Sudan, South Sudan, and Zambia — working alongside the United Nations and international civil society. That experience is not biographical decoration. It is the ground from which every argument he writes ultimately rises.

He has worked with landmine survivors, internally displaced persons, and refugees. He has sat in protection cluster meetings in Juba and Kampala. He has written field reports that became policy. He has seen what international law looks like — and what it fails to look like — when it leaves the textbook and enters the camp.

Field Deployments

    Independence & After

Guiding Principles

How He Works

01

Community-Led

Humanitarian response that centres the agency and dignity of affected communities, not the convenience of external actors.

02

Rights-Based

Every intervention grounded in international humanitarian law and human rights frameworks — not charity, but entitlement.

03

Evidence-Driven

Field data and community testimony as the foundation of programme design, advocacy, and policy engagement.

04

Locally Accountable

Accountability to affected populations as the primary measure of success — not donor reporting cycles or headquarters metrics.
